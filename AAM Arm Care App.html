Section 1
WHY ARM CARE MATTERS
The Throwing Chain

Throwing is one of the most complex and physically demanding movements in sport. In efficient throwing, force is generated from the ground and transferred through the hips, trunk, and shoulder before being expressed through the arm.

The arm functions primarily as a stress receiver and force transmitter, not a primary force generator. When movement quality, strength, or control is lacking elsewhere in the system, the arm is forced to compensate — leading to increased joint stress and a higher likelihood of breakdown.

Legs → Hips → Core → Shoulder → Arm → Ball

Fatigue Is a Primary Risk Factor

Fatigue is one of the strongest predictors of injury in throwing athletes. As fatigue accumulates:

  • Movement patterns become less efficient
  • Muscle activation timing is delayed
  • Joint loads increase
  • Recovery between sessions declines

Many throwing injuries are not caused by a single event, but by repeated exposure to stress once the body's ability to adapt has diminished.

Preparation Drives Performance

Arm care is not a limitation on performance — it is a prerequisite for it. Athletes who consistently prioritize preparation demonstrate:

  • Improved throwing efficiency
  • Greater consistency in output
  • Enhanced body awareness and control
  • Increased confidence in physical readiness
Long-Term Athletic Development

Throwing success is built over years, not weeks. Sustainable development requires an approach that respects workload, recovery, and progression. Short-term gains achieved without proper preparation often come at the expense of long-term health.

Core Philosophy The goal is not to eliminate stress, but to ensure the athlete is equipped to tolerate it.
Section 2
AAM ARM CARE STANDARDS
Core Standards
  • Train intelligently and manage stress to support long-term performance and durability.
  • Arm care is not about doing more — it's about doing the right things, consistently.
  • Small, consistent improvements produce the greatest long-term results.
  • Arm care supports throwing and should enhance training, not compete with it.
  • Ignoring warning signs is not toughness — it increases risk.
Standards in Practice
Preparation before intensity
Arm care prepares the body to throw efficiently rather than fatiguing it before high-stress work.
Stress is managed, not avoided
Throwing stress is necessary for development but must be appropriately dosed and supported.
Progressions are respected
Intensity and volume advance when capacity improves — not simply because time has passed.
Quality over quantity
More work is not better work. Intent and execution matter more than volume.
Feedback guides adjustment
Soreness, fatigue, and movement changes are treated as information — not obstacles to ignore.
Consistency builds durability
Small actions performed consistently produce the greatest long-term benefit.
Section 3
RED FLAGS & ATHLETE RESPONSIBILITIES
Stop Immediately If you experience any of the following, STOP the program and seek evaluation from an AAM provider or qualified medical professional.
Pain-Related Red Flags
  • Sharp, stabbing, or sudden pain
  • Pain that worsens during or after throwing
  • Pain that does not improve with rest or recovery
  • Pain that alters mechanics or movement patterns
Neurological Symptoms
  • Numbness or tingling in the arm, hand, or fingers
  • Burning sensations
  • Loss of coordination or grip strength
Range of Motion or Strength Changes
  • Sudden or progressive loss of shoulder or elbow range of motion
  • Noticeable weakness not related to normal fatigue
  • Inability to fully straighten or bend the elbow
Swelling & Structural Concerns
  • Visible swelling around the elbow or shoulder
  • Unusual tightness or pressure that does not resolve
  • Audible popping or catching accompanied by pain
Fatigue That Does Not Resolve
  • Persistent arm heaviness
  • Loss of throwing feel or control over multiple sessions
  • Declining performance despite adequate rest
The Athlete's Responsibilities
  • Train with awareness and intent
  • Respect fatigue and recovery
  • Perform all movements with control and purpose
  • Communicate symptoms or concerns early
  • Avoid pushing through pain in pursuit of performance
  • Show up consistently and prepared

Youth Guide
YOUTH ARM CARE
For Little League athletes and parents.
AAM Philosophy for Youth We Train to Throw — Not Throw to Train. A strong arm starts with a strong body.
Our 5 Youth Arm Care Rules
1. Pain is NOT normal
Soreness after activity can happen. Pain while throwing is different. If your child says their elbow hurts, arm feels dead, or it hurts when they throw — stop throwing immediately.
2. Fatigue changes everything
Tired athletes move differently. When mechanics break down, injury risk goes up. Signs: throwing slower, missing targets, dropping elbow, arm feels heavy.
3. More throwing is NOT better
Kids do not improve by endlessly throwing. Smart training beats extra volume. Quality over quantity.
4. Recovery is part of training
Drink water, get sleep, eat protein + balanced meals, take rest days. Recovery helps the arm rebuild.
5. Speak up early
Small issues become big problems when ignored. Early evaluation = faster return + fewer missed games.
When to Stop Throwing Immediately
  • Sharp pain
  • Tingling or numbness
  • Sudden loss of velocity
  • Loss of control
  • "Dead arm" feeling
  • Swelling
  • Pain that lasts after rest
Simple Game Day Checklist
  • Did I sleep well?
  • Did I eat?
  • Did I warm up?
  • Does my arm feel normal?
If any answer is NO Talk to an adult before throwing.
Parents: Your Role
  • Help your athlete listen to their body
  • Be honest about pain
  • Respect rest days
  • Avoid "playing through it"
  • Communicate with coaches/providers

The goal is long-term development — not just this weekend's game.

Stop Throwing When You're Warm Between catch play, long toss, plyo ball work, and pre-game bullpens, athletes can easily accumulate 100–150 throws before competition begins. Those throws count. Wasted throws add unnecessary stress and fatigue before performance even starts.
Long Toss: Distance & Volume

Long toss can be valuable when it reinforces quality movement. Throwing on a line matters — excessive launch angles create poor habits. Distance should not compromise mechanics.

For most athletes, long toss should not exceed 120–150 feet. Quality of movement always takes priority over distance.

Throwing farther is not always throwing better.

Weighted Balls: When and Who Should Use Them

Weighted ball training has become one of the most discussed — and most misused — tools in baseball development. When implemented correctly with appropriate athletes, it can support velocity development, range of motion, and arm strength. When implemented incorrectly, it significantly increases injury risk.

Eligibility This protocol is intended for athletes 16 years of age and older who have completed a full baseline assessment, are currently pain-free, have no history of elbow or shoulder injury within the past 12 months, and are under the supervision of a qualified professional.

Annual Throwing Volume & Individualization

Within a one-year timeframe, a commonly accepted guideline for pitchers is approximately 100 innings or 10 months of throwing. Every throwing year must include a deload period and a structured build-up phase. There is no universal timeline — only intelligent progression.

Reality Check There is no such thing as a year-round throwing athlete who stays healthy without planned rest. Every successful professional pitcher has an off-season. Your athlete should too.

Multi-Sport & Travel Ball Considerations
  • Pitching for more than one team in the same week without coordinated pitch count tracking is the leading cause of youth arm injury
  • Showcase events often involve multiple short outings in a single day — each should count toward weekly totals
  • Off-season showcases held in August–October give athletes insufficient recovery time before spring seasons
  • Encourage athletes and parents to communicate with all coaches about total workload
  • When total annual throwing volume is unknown, default to conservative limits and a structured build-up
Real-World Example A 17-year-old who pitches for his high school team, a travel team, and attends two showcases in the same month may throw more in 30 days than some professional pitchers do. This is a medical concern, not just a performance one.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
Common questions from athletes and parents.
Why can't my athlete throw every day? ▼
Throwing places repetitive stress on the shoulder and elbow. After a throwing session, the tissues in the arm — tendons, ligaments, muscles, and cartilage — undergo microtrauma that requires time to repair. When throwing occurs before this repair is complete, stress accumulates faster than the body can adapt. Rest days are not wasted days — they are when the adaptation actually happens.
My athlete says the arm feels fine — why limit pitch count? ▼
Feeling fine is not the same as being recovered. Many arm injuries — including UCL tears and stress reactions — develop gradually over weeks or months before any pain appears. By the time pain is present, significant damage may already have occurred. Pitch count limits exist because the research is clear: athletes who exceed them have dramatically higher injury rates, regardless of how they feel.
When is my athlete ready to pitch again after taking time off? ▼
Return to pitching after a layoff requires a structured build-up program — not just a few days of catch. For a standard offseason shutdown, athletes typically need 6–8 weeks of progressive throwing before they are ready to pitch competitively. Following an injury, that timeline depends on the nature and severity of the injury and requires medical clearance.
What does normal arm soreness look like after pitching? ▼
Mild muscle soreness that peaks at 24 hours and resolves by 36–48 hours is generally normal. What is NOT normal: sharp or stabbing pain during throwing, soreness that gets progressively worse after each outing, soreness in the joint rather than the muscles, pain that requires extended warm-ups to resolve, or numbness/tingling in the arm or hand.
Is year-round baseball a problem? ▼
Yes — when it eliminates the opportunity for structured rest and recovery. Athletes who throw for 10 or more months per year without a meaningful break have significantly higher rates of elbow and shoulder injury. The issue is not playing baseball year-round — it is throwing year-round without periodized rest. A structured shutdown of 4–8 weeks followed by a proper build-up dramatically reduces cumulative stress.
Should my athlete play other sports? ▼
Yes — especially in adolescence. Multi-sport participation through high school has been associated with lower overuse injury rates, higher levels of athletic development, and longer athletic careers. Sport specialization before age 14 is consistently linked to higher injury risk and earlier burnout.
Are weighted balls safe for my athlete? ▼
Weighted ball training carries meaningful injury risk, particularly for younger athletes and those with any history of arm injury. AAM's position is that weighted balls may be appropriate for athletes 16 and older who are pain-free, have adequate arm strength and mobility, and are supervised by a qualified professional. They are not appropriate for athletes under 16, those with active symptoms, or those without a proper foundation of arm care and strength.
What is the difference between arm soreness and arm pain? ▼
Soreness is a diffuse, muscular ache that typically develops 12–24 hours after activity and is not present during throwing. Pain is present during activity, is often focal (in a specific spot), and may be sharp, stabbing, or burning. Soreness is a normal training response. Pain is a signal that something is wrong. If it hurts during throwing — stop, do not push through, and seek evaluation.
Should my athlete ice after every outing? ▼
Icing after throwing is common and effective for managing soreness and discomfort. However, it should not be applied for more than 15 minutes and should not be used directly before throwing, as it can reduce proprioception and tissue extensibility. Post-throw stretching and band work are at least as important as icing for long-term arm health.
What should I do if my athlete complains of elbow or shoulder pain? ▼
Stop throwing immediately. Do not have the athlete push through pain in hopes it resolves. Schedule an evaluation with a sports medicine physician or orthopedic specialist — not after the season, now. Early evaluation of throwing-related pain dramatically improves outcomes and reduces the likelihood of a more serious injury.
What is GIRD and why does it matter? ▼
GIRD stands for Glenohumeral Internal Rotation Deficit — a loss of internal rotation range of motion in the throwing shoulder compared to the non-throwing side. A mild degree of GIRD is normal in throwing athletes. However, when GIRD exceeds 18–20°, it is associated with increased shoulder and elbow injury risk. GIRD is addressed through posterior shoulder stretching (sleeper stretch, cross-body stretch).
